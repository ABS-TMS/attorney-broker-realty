The First Two Years

This is the window that actually decides whether you stay in this business. Here's what it really looks like — and why most new agents don't make it to their first renewal with a system that could've kept them.

01

The money is brutal, and nobody tells you upfront

New agents earn a median of just $8,100 in their first two years. 62% make less than $10,000. That's not a "some agents struggle" statistic — that's most new agents, and it lands right as your first license renewal comes due. (NAR 2025 Member Profile)

02

"Sink or swim" isn't a training philosophy — it's the absence of one

Most brokerages hand you a welcome packet, a login, and a "call if you need anything." Then the actual job — showings, negotiations, compliance, follow-up — has to be figured out alone, usually while your first deals are falling apart.

03

The failure isn't sudden — it's slow, and quiet

It rarely looks like quitting. It looks like a licensee who stops returning calls, picks up other work by the fall, and lets the license quietly go inactive the following spring. Nobody decides to fail. They just never had a system that made the daily habits automatic.

Daily Compliance Training AI-Graded

Not a PDF you skim once a year. A daily habit that keeps you fluent in the current TREC 20-19 form, graded in real time — with a direct line to your broker the moment something doesn't make sense.

Sample scenario — Day 12

"Your buyer is purchasing as an LLC. The title company asks who's responsible for providing beneficial ownership information under Paragraph 20B — and who pays for it?"

Agent's answer: "The buyer provides it since they're the one forming the LLC."

AI feedback: Right that it's the buyer's obligation — but under 20-19, the buyer also pays any charges tied to gathering and reporting that information. Worth flagging to entity buyers early, not at the closing table...
